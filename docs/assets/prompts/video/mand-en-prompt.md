# Mand (EN) — Video Generation Prompt

Create a 2-3 minute educational video for parents of toddlers (0-18 months) explaining the concept of requesting — when a child learns to ask for what they want.

## Audience

Parents, not specialists. Use warm, supportive, conversational language. Never use clinical or professional terminology — no "ABA," "verbal operant," "reinforcement," "stimulus," "mand," or any technical jargon. Speak as if explaining to a caring parent over coffee. The parent should feel informed and empowered, never judged or overwhelmed.

## Visual Style

Hand-drawn animated illustration style. Soft, warm color palette (cream, peach, soft green, gentle blue). Simple line-drawn characters — a toddler and a parent. Clean, uncluttered scenes. Think whiteboard animation meets children's book illustration. No text-heavy screens — let the visuals tell the story. When showing speech, use small, simple speech bubbles.

## Structure — Follow This Exact Scene Sequence

### SCENE 1 — HOOK (0:00–0:15)

A toddler reaches toward a cookie jar on a high shelf. They look up at their parent. Frustration on their face. Then they say "cookie!" (show a small speech bubble). The parent smiles, reaches for the jar, hands the cookie. The child beams.

Narration: something like "Have you ever seen this moment? Your child wants something — and finds a way to tell you. This is requesting, and it's one of the most important skills your child will ever learn."

### SCENE 2 — WHY IT MATTERS (0:15–0:50)

Show a split view. Left side: a child crying, parent guessing what's wrong (question marks floating above). Right side: the same child pointing and saying a word, parent understanding immediately (light bulb appears).

Narrate why requesting is the child's first real control over their world. Emphasize: the motivation is built in because the child already wants things.

Then show a simple circular loop animation:

- Child communicates (speech bubble)
- → Gets the item (item appears)
- → Wants to communicate more (circular arrows back to start)

Key line: "Every time your child asks and gets what they want, they learn: communicating works."

### SCENE 3 — WHAT IT IS (0:50–1:15)

Simple animated examples. Show the toddler in different everyday moments:

- Saying "ball" while reaching for a ball
- Saying "juice" at the kitchen table
- Saying "book" while holding up a book toward parent
- Saying "swing" at the playground

Each example appears as a quick, clean vignette (3-4 seconds each).

Then briefly show that ANY form of communication counts:

- A spoken word (speech bubble)
- A gesture/point (hand pointing)
- A picture card (card being handed over)

Narration: "Requesting can be a word, a gesture, or even a picture card. What matters is your child is intentionally communicating."

### SCENE 4 — HOW IT GROWS (1:15–2:00)

Draw a gentle staircase with 5 steps. The toddler character climbs up one step at a time.

**Step 1 — "With help":** Parent says the word, child repeats. Show the number 2 (items). Brief animation: parent says "cookie," child echoes "cookie!" Narration: "At first, your child might need you to say the word so they can repeat it. That's perfectly normal — it's how learning starts."

**Step 2 — "On their own":** Child says the word without help. Show the number 4 (items). Brief animation: child sees ball and says "ball!" on their own.

**Step 3 — "Different people and places":** Show child with mom, then with grandma, then at the park (3 quick frames). Show the number 6 (items). Narration: "Can they ask grandma at the park? When the skill works everywhere, it's truly learned."

**Step 4 — "Spontaneously":** Child initiates on their own, no cues. Show a small clock with 60 minutes. Animation: child sees something, turns to parent, asks on their own.

**Step 5 — "Rich vocabulary":** Fan of 10 different items arranged in a circle around the child (cookie, ball, juice, book, swing, car, apple, and others).

Narration: "This is a journey, and every child moves through it at their own pace."

### SCENE 5 — ENCOURAGEMENT + CTA (2:00–2:30)

Brief parent tips shown as gentle icons with short captions:

- Hands helping icon: "Prompts are normal"
- Star icon: "Any attempt counts"
- Clock/sun icon: "Practice during everyday moments"

Transition to a warm, inviting closing screen:
"Ready to see where your child is? Start the assessment."
Show a friendly forward arrow or inviting button graphic.

## Tone

Warm, encouraging, normalizing. Use phrases like:

- "It's perfectly normal"
- "Every child moves at their own pace"
- "Even small attempts matter"
- "Prompts are how learning starts"

NEVER make parents feel their child is behind, failing, or that they're doing something wrong. This video should feel like a supportive friend explaining something helpful, not a clinical evaluation.

## Pacing

Gentle, not rushed. Let visuals breathe. Brief pauses between scenes. The 2-3 minute duration should feel comfortable, not crammed.

## Narration Language

English.

## Important Notes

- Do NOT use any of these terms: ABA, mand, verbal operant, reinforcement, stimulus, generalization, repertoire, echoic
- DO use plain language equivalents: "requesting," "asking," "with help/on their own," "different people and places," "vocabulary"
- Examples shown visually must be: cookie, ball, book, juice, swing (these must match the Russian version exactly)
- The visual character design, scene layout, and animation style must be identical to the Russian version of this video — only the narration language changes
