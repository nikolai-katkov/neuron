# Mand (Requesting) — Complete Reference for Video Creation

## Context

This document provides all domain knowledge needed to create a 2-3 minute educational video about requesting skills for parents of toddlers aged 0-18 months. The video is part of a developmental assessment app that helps parents understand and evaluate their child's communication milestones.

The app flow: Parent watches intro video → Understands the skill → Starts assessment (5 yes/no questions) → If "no", gets training guidance → Reassesses.

This video serves as the intro — it should leave parents feeling informed, confident, and ready to begin the assessment.

---

## What Is Requesting?

Requesting is when your child uses communication to ask for something they want. It's one of the first and most important communication skills a child develops.

Examples of requesting in everyday life:

- Your child says "cookie" when they see the cookie jar
- They point at the swing and say "swing"
- They bring you a book and say "book" (meaning "read to me")
- They say "juice" when thirsty
- They pull you toward the door and say "outside"
- A child reaches for a toy on a high shelf and says "ball"

Requesting includes ANY intentional communication: spoken words, sign language, gestures, pointing, or picture card exchange (PECS cards — cards with pictures that children hand to adults to communicate).

## Why Requesting Is So Important

### It's the child's first real control

Before requesting, a child's only options are crying or waiting for you to guess what they need. Requesting gives them direct control over their world — they can tell you what they want, and you can respond. This is empowering for the child and transformative for the parent-child relationship.

### The motivation is built in

Unlike other communication skills (like naming things or repeating sounds), requesting is driven by the child's own desire. They WANT something, which means they're naturally motivated to figure out how to communicate. This makes it the ideal starting point for language development.

### Success is immediate and tangible

When a child successfully requests, they get what they asked for right away. This creates a powerful learning loop:

1. Child wants something
2. Child communicates (word, gesture, card)
3. Child gets the item
4. Child learns: "communicating works!"
5. Child is motivated to communicate again

This loop strengthens with every successful request, building the foundation for all future communication.

### It builds confidence

Every successful request teaches the child that their communication has power. This confidence carries over into other areas of language development.

## How Requesting Develops — The Five Stages

Children develop requesting skills in a predictable progression across three dimensions:

- **Independence**: from needing help to doing it completely on their own
- **Generalization**: from one person/place to many people and places
- **Vocabulary size**: from 2 items to 10+ items

### Stage 1: Requesting with help (2 items)

**What it looks like:** The child can request at least 2 items, but needs your help. You might say the word first so they can repeat it, show them a gesture to copy, or present a picture card for them to hand to you.

**Why this matters:** This is where the learning loop begins. The child starts to understand that communication leads to getting what they want. Prompts (help) are completely normal and expected at this stage.

**What parents can do:**

- Choose 2 items your child loves and is motivated to get
- Hold the item where your child can see it but can't reach it (e.g., in a clear container)
- Say the name clearly (e.g., "cookie") and wait for any attempt to repeat or gesture
- As soon as your child makes ANY attempt to request, give the item immediately and praise them
- Practice during natural moments: mealtimes, play, getting dressed
- Keep sessions short (5-10 minutes), always end on a success

**Common mistakes to avoid:**

- Giving the item before the child makes any communicative attempt
- Using physical hand-over-hand prompts when a verbal or gestural prompt would work
- Only practicing during structured sessions instead of natural moments
- Waiting too long to give the item after a successful request (should be immediate)

### Stage 2: Requesting independently (4 items)

**What it looks like:** The child makes at least 4 different requests without any prompts from you. The desired item can be visible — what matters is that the child initiates the communication on their own.

**Why this matters:** This is the leap from "repeating what you said" to "choosing to communicate." The child decides on their own to make a request. This is a significant milestone in independence.

**What parents can do:**

- Place desired items in sight but out of reach
- Give small portions so the child needs to ask for more
- Start a favorite activity and pause, waiting for the child to request continuation
- Wait for the child to initiate — do NOT ask "What do you want?" (that counts as a prompt)
- Immediately deliver the item and give specific praise ("Great job saying ball!")

**Common mistakes to avoid:**

- Asking "What do you want?" (this is a prompt and counts as help)
- Not creating enough opportunities for the child to need to request
- Counting the same request multiple times instead of tracking unique ones
- Expecting full sentences — single words or clear gestures count

### Stage 3: Requesting with different people and in different places (6 items)

**What it looks like:** The child can request with at least 2 different people (e.g., mom and grandma), in at least 2 different environments (e.g., home and park), and for at least 2 types of items (e.g., food and toys). Total: 6 requests across these different combinations.

**Why this matters:** A child might ask mom for juice at home perfectly, but can they ask grandma at the park? When the skill works across different people, places, and items, it means the skill is truly learned — not just a habit tied to one specific situation.

**What parents can do:**

- Practice with a second caregiver (other parent, grandparent, sibling)
- Try requesting in a new environment (park, car, store)
- Vary the items so the child requests different categories (food, toys, activities)
- Change one thing at a time: first new person (same place), then new place (same person)
- Keep a log: who, where, what — to track the combinations

**Common mistakes to avoid:**

- Only practicing at home with the same caregiver
- Changing too many variables at once (new person AND new place AND new items)
- Not tracking which combinations have been achieved
- Giving up if the child struggles in a new setting — help and try again

### Stage 4: Spontaneous requesting (5 requests in 60 minutes)

**What it looks like:** The child makes at least 5 requests during a 60-minute observation period, completely on their own initiative. No verbal cues, no questions, no gestures from you. They see or think of something they want, and they ask for it.

**Why this matters:** This is the biggest leap — from responding to opportunities to creating them. The child doesn't need ANY external trigger to communicate. This shows that requesting has become a natural, automatic part of how they interact with the world.

**What parents can do:**

- Set up a 60-minute period during a natural routine (playtime, mealtime)
- Ensure motivating items are accessible but require the child to request
- Do NOT prompt, suggest, or point at items — let the child initiate everything
- When the child makes a request, fulfill it promptly and praise
- Record each spontaneous request with the time

**Common mistakes to avoid:**

- Inadvertently prompting by looking at items or gesturing toward them
- Choosing an observation time when the child isn't motivated (e.g., right after a big meal)
- Not having enough motivating items available
- Counting echoic responses (child repeating something you said) as spontaneous

### Stage 5: A rich vocabulary of requests (10 independent requests)

**What it looks like:** The child can independently request at least 10 different things without any prompts. These span different categories: foods, toys, activities, help, social actions.

**Why this matters:** This represents a flexible, functional communication system. The child isn't limited to asking for one or two things — they have a rich vocabulary of requests that covers their daily needs and interests.

**What parents can do:**

- List all items your child currently requests independently
- Identify gaps: what motivating items has the child not yet learned to request?
- Introduce new items one at a time, using prompts initially and fading them
- Good categories to expand: foods (apple, juice, cracker), toys (ball, car, blocks), activities (swing, tickle, music), social (up, open, help)
- Keep a running tally

**Common mistakes to avoid:**

- Counting prompted requests as independent
- Teaching too many new words at once instead of one at a time
- Not fading prompts gradually (jumping from full prompt to no prompt)
- Only counting requests during structured teaching rather than throughout the day

## Key Messages for Parents

These messages should come through naturally in the video:

1. **"It's perfectly normal"** — Children develop at different rates. Being at different stages for different skills is expected and okay.

2. **"Prompts are a normal part of learning"** — Help is not failure. The goal is to gradually reduce help over time, not to eliminate it immediately.

3. **"Any attempt counts"** — A word, a gesture, a point, a card — all are valid forms of communication. The form doesn't matter; the intention does.

4. **"Practice in everyday moments"** — Meals, play, getting dressed, going outside. Short natural moments work better than long formal sessions.

5. **"Always respond"** — When your child tries to communicate, acknowledge and reward the attempt, even if it's imperfect.

6. **"Every child moves at their own pace"** — This is a journey, not a race. The stages show the direction, not a timeline.
