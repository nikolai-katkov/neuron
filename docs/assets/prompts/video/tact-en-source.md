# Tact (Labeling) — Complete Reference for Video Creation

## Context

This document provides all domain knowledge needed to create a 2-3 minute educational video about labeling skills for parents of toddlers aged 0-18 months. The video is part of a developmental assessment app that helps parents understand and evaluate their child's communication milestones.

The app flow: Parent watches intro video → Understands the skill → Starts assessment (5 yes/no questions) → If "no", gets training guidance → Reassesses.

This video serves as the intro — it should leave parents feeling informed, confident, and ready to begin the assessment.

## What Is Labeling?

Labeling is when your child names something they see, hear, smell, or touch — not because they want it, but because they notice it. It's a fundamental communication skill that connects words to the world around your child.

Examples of labeling in everyday life:

- Your child sees the family dog and says "dog" (not because they want the dog)
- They point at your shoe and say "shoe"
- They look at a picture in a book and say "cat"
- They see mama come into the room and say "mama"
- While playing, they hold up a ball and say "ball" — just naming it
- They spot a bird outside the window and say "bird"

## The Difference Between Requesting and Labeling

This is the key distinction parents need to understand:

**Requesting:** Your child says "ball" because they want to play with the ball. They're motivated by wanting the item. The communication is driven by desire.

**Labeling:** Your child sees a ball on the shelf and says "ball" just because they noticed it. They're motivated by seeing the item. The communication is driven by observation.

Same word. Completely different motivation.

Think of it this way:

- Requesting = "I want that" → communicating needs
- Labeling = "I see that and know what it's called" → communicating about the world

## Why Labeling Is So Important

### It's the bridge from needs to language

Requesting lets your child communicate what they want. Labeling lets them communicate about the world. This is a huge cognitive leap — the child moves from "I need things" to "I understand things."

### It builds vocabulary

Every label is a new word connected to a real object or person. Unlike requesting (which is limited to things the child wants), labeling can apply to ANYTHING the child notices. This opens up an unlimited vocabulary.

### It shows understanding

When a child labels something, they're demonstrating that they recognize it and can categorize it. Saying "dog" when they see a dog means they know what a dog is, not just that they want one.

### It enables conversation

Once a child can name things, they can talk about them. Labeling is the prerequisite for describing, storytelling, and eventually conversation. It transforms language from a tool for getting things into a tool for sharing experiences.

### It creates shared moments

When your child points at something and names it, they're inviting you to share their experience. "Look, a dog!" creates a moment of connection — you both look at the dog together. These shared attention moments are powerful for bonding and further language development.

### It's the bridge from practical to rich language

- Practical communication: "I want juice" (requesting)
- Rich language: "Look, a bird!" (labeling)

Labeling is what takes a child from functional communication to truly rich, expressive language.

## How Labeling Develops — The Five Stages

Children develop labeling skills in a predictable progression across three dimensions:

- **Vocabulary size**: from 2 items to 10+ items across categories
- **Complexity**: from favorite items to everyday objects (harder because less motivating)
- **Independence**: from labeling when asked to spontaneous labeling

### Stage 1: Naming 2 favorite things

**What it looks like:** The child can name at least 2 objects when shown them. These are typically things they love and see often — mama, papa, a favorite toy, a pet.

**Why this matters:** Children naturally label things they feel emotionally connected to first. These first labels are the foundation for all future vocabulary.

**What parents can do:**

- Choose 2 items your child loves and can already request (the words are already in their vocabulary)
- Hold up the item and ask "What is this?" in a clear, enthusiastic voice
- If the child doesn't answer, say the name yourself (e.g., "ball!") and wait for them to repeat
- When they say the name, give enthusiastic praise and a small reward
- Start with real objects, not pictures

**Common mistakes to avoid:**

- Using only flashcards or pictures instead of real, familiar objects
- Saying the answer too quickly without giving the child time to respond (wait 3-5 seconds)
- Practicing only during structured sessions rather than natural moments
- Choosing items the child has never seen before instead of familiar favorites

### Stage 2: Naming 4 things

**What it looks like:** The child can name at least 4 objects including people, animals, or characters when directly tested.

**Why this matters:** The vocabulary is expanding. The child is connecting more words to more things in their world.

**What parents can do:**

- Mix favorites (teddy bear, mama) with familiar everyday items (cup, shoe)
- Show one item at a time and ask "What is this?" Wait 3-5 seconds
- If the child needs help, say just the first sound of the word (e.g., "b..." for ball) instead of the full word
- Alternate between items the child knows well and newer ones
- Build one new label at a time alongside known items — don't teach all 4 at once

**Common mistakes to avoid:**

- Trying to teach all new labels at once instead of adding one at a time
- Not fading prompts gradually (jumping from full prompt to no prompt)
- Testing only with pictures when the child learns better with real objects
- Skipping practice with already-known items (reviewing known words builds confidence)

### Stage 3: Naming 6 everyday objects

**What it looks like:** The child can name at least 6 ordinary, everyday objects that are NOT their favorites. These are items they may not be especially interested in — a shoe, a hat, a spoon, a cup, a car, a bed.

**Why this matters:** This is a critical milestone. The child's language is growing beyond just things they want. They can now name ordinary things in their environment, showing that their vocabulary isn't limited by motivation. This is harder for the child precisely because they're less excited about these items.

**What parents can do:**

- Gather 6 everyday objects from around the house
- Use a reward system: give a sticker or token for each correct label, collect tokens to earn a favorite item
- After each correct label, briefly distract the child (clap, sing a line), then ask about the same object again
- Give 1 token for saying just the word ("spoon"), and 2 tokens for a phrase ("This is a spoon") to encourage fuller answers
- Practice with real household objects the child encounters daily

**Common mistakes to avoid:**

- Skipping non-preferred items and only practicing with favorites
- Not using a reward system (motivation is lower for everyday objects, rewards help)
- Moving to new words before the child consistently names current ones after distractions
- Forgetting to make the reward exchange motivating (tokens should lead to something the child really wants)

### Stage 4: Spontaneous labeling

**What it looks like:** The child starts naming things on their own without being asked "What is this?" They see a dog and say "dog!" without any prompting. Observed during a 60-minute period of natural activity.

**Why this matters:** This is the leap from "answering a question" to "sharing an observation." The child is now using language to communicate about the world on their own initiative, not just responding to your prompts. This shows labeling has become a natural, automatic behavior.

**What parents can do:**

- Set up a 60-minute observation during a natural routine (play, walk, mealtime)
- Fill the environment with interesting objects but don't point at or name anything yourself
- When the child spontaneously names something, respond with enthusiastic praise ("Yes, that IS a dog! Great noticing!")
- Be present and responsive without prompting — let the child lead
- Note each spontaneous label with the time

**Common mistakes to avoid:**

- Inadvertently prompting by pointing at objects or looking at them expectantly
- Choosing an observation time when the child is tired or not engaged
- Not having enough interesting objects or scenery to spark spontaneous labeling
- Counting labels that came after you asked a question or named something first

### Stage 5: Naming 10 objects from different categories

**What it looks like:** The child can name at least 10 objects spanning different categories: household items, people, body parts, pictures.

**Why this matters:** This represents a broad, flexible vocabulary. The child can name things from multiple areas of their world, not just one category. This diversity shows robust language development.

**What parents can do:**

- List all objects your child currently labels — identify which categories are missing
- Categories to cover: household items (cup, chair), people (mom, dad), body parts (nose, hand), pictures in books
- Introduce new words one category at a time, starting with simple, common items
- Check mastery: the child labels the item correctly for 3 days in a row without help
- Keep a running count across categories

**Common mistakes to avoid:**

- Teaching too many new words at once
- Not checking mastery before counting a word as learned (3 consecutive days without help)
- Focusing on only one category instead of spreading across different areas
- Counting labels that still require a prompt as independent

## Key Messages for Parents

These messages should come through naturally in the video:

1. **"It's perfectly normal"** — Children develop at different rates. Naming favorites before everyday items is the natural progression.

2. **"Start with what they know"** — If your child can request an item (like a ball), they can probably label it too. Build on existing vocabulary.

3. **"Use real objects first"** — Real objects are easier to learn from than pictures. Move to pictures after the child can label the real thing.

4. **"Give time before helping"** — Count to 3-5 in your head before providing the answer. Children need processing time.

5. **"Praise every attempt"** — Even if the pronunciation isn't perfect, acknowledge the effort. Close approximations count.

6. **"Keep it playful"** — Labeling happens naturally during play, walks, mealtimes, bath time. Don't make it feel like a test.

7. **"Every child moves at their own pace"** — The stages show direction, not a timeline.
