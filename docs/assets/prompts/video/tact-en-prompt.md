# Tact (EN) — Video Generation Prompt

Create a 2-3 minute educational video for parents of toddlers (0-18 months) explaining the concept of labeling — when a child learns to name things they see in the world.

## Audience

Parents, not specialists. Use warm, supportive, conversational language. Never use clinical or professional terminology — no "ABA," "verbal operant," "tact," "stimulus," "non-reinforcing," or any technical jargon. Speak as if explaining to a caring parent over coffee. The parent should feel informed and empowered, never judged or overwhelmed.

## Visual Style

Hand-drawn animated illustration style. Soft, warm color palette (cream, peach, soft green, gentle blue). Simple line-drawn characters — a toddler and a parent. Clean, uncluttered scenes. Think whiteboard animation meets children's book illustration. No text-heavy screens — let the visuals tell the story. When showing speech, use small, simple speech bubbles.

## Structure — Follow This Exact Scene Sequence

### SCENE 1 — HOOK (0:00–0:15)

A toddler and parent walking in a park. The toddler spots a dog, points, and says "dog!" (speech bubble). The parent looks down, smiles, says "Yes, that's a dog!" Both look at the dog together — a warm, shared moment.

Narration: something like "There's a magical moment in every child's development — when they stop just asking for things and start naming the world around them. This is labeling, and it changes everything."

### SCENE 2 — WHY IT MATTERS (0:15–0:50)

First, show the concept visually: draw simple objects (dog, shoe, ball, cup) with dotted lines connecting each object to its name floating nearby. Words connecting to things — that's what labeling is.

Then show the KEY difference from requesting with a split screen:

- Left side: child says "ball" and reaches for it eagerly (wanting — a heart icon above)
- Right side: child sees a ball on a shelf and says "ball" calmly, just to name it (noticing — an eye icon above)

Narrate: "Requesting is 'I want that.' Labeling is 'I see that and I know what it's called.' Same word, completely different reason."

Then the big insight — show a light bulb or spark moment:
"When your child labels something, they're not just communicating needs anymore — they're communicating about the world. They're saying: 'I understand this.' That's a huge leap."

Brief visual: practical communication ("I want juice" — arrow to juice) transforms into rich language ("Look, a bird!" — shared moment looking up at sky).

### SCENE 3 — WHAT IT IS (0:50–1:15)

Simple animated examples. Show the toddler in different everyday moments:

- Pointing at a dog and saying "dog" (at the park)
- Holding up a shoe and saying "shoe" (at home)
- Looking at a book with a cat picture and saying "cat"
- Seeing mama enter the room and saying "mama"
- Holding a ball and saying "ball" — just naming it, not asking for it

Each example appears as a quick, clean vignette (3-4 seconds each).

Narration: "Your child names things not because they want them, but because they notice them. It's how they share their world with you."

### SCENE 4 — HOW IT GROWS (1:15–2:00)

Draw a gentle staircase with 5 steps. The toddler character climbs up one step at a time.

**Step 1 — "2 favorites":** Show mama and a teddy bear. Narration: "Children usually start by naming things they love — mama, papa, a favorite toy. These first labels are the foundation."

**Step 2 — "4 things":** Add a dog and a cat next to mama and teddy bear. The vocabulary is growing.

**Step 3 — "6 everyday objects":** Show ordinary items appearing one by one: shoe, hat, spoon, cup, car, bed. Narration: "Then something interesting happens — they start naming things that aren't their favorites. A shoe. A spoon. This is harder, because they're less excited about these items. But it shows their language is really growing."

**Step 4 — "Spontaneous naming":** Child names things without being asked. Show a clock with 60 minutes. Animation: child notices an object and names it on their own, unprompted. Narration: "The next leap: your child names things without you asking 'What is this?' They just notice and name."

**Step 5 — "10 from different categories":** Fan of items from different groups (household, people, body parts, pictures) arranged around the child. Narration: "The goal: naming things from all parts of their world."

Final narration: "This is a journey, and every child moves through it at their own pace."

### SCENE 5 — ENCOURAGEMENT + CTA (2:00–2:30)

Brief parent tips shown as gentle icons with short captions:

- Building blocks icon: "Start with things they already know"
- Real object icon (3D cube): "Use real objects first"
- Star/heart icon: "Praise every attempt"

Transition to a warm, inviting closing screen:
"Ready to see where your child is? Start the assessment."
Show a friendly forward arrow or inviting button graphic.

## Tone

Warm, encouraging, normalizing. Use phrases like:

- "It's perfectly normal"
- "Every child moves at their own pace"
- "Even small attempts matter"
- "It's natural for children to name favorites first"

NEVER make parents feel their child is behind, failing, or that they're doing something wrong. This video should feel like a supportive friend explaining something helpful, not a clinical evaluation.

## Pacing

Gentle, not rushed. Let visuals breathe. Brief pauses between scenes. The 2-3 minute duration should feel comfortable, not crammed.

## Narration Language

English.

## Important Notes

- Do NOT use any of these terms: ABA, tact, verbal operant, reinforcement, stimulus, non-reinforcing, repertoire, token economy, cold probe
- DO use plain language equivalents: "labeling," "naming," "favorites vs. everyday objects," "spontaneous," "vocabulary"
- Examples shown visually must be: dog, shoe, hat, spoon, cup, ball, cat, mama (these must match the Russian version exactly)
- The split-screen comparison (requesting vs. labeling) is the MOST important visual — spend time on it
- The visual character design, scene layout, and animation style must be identical to the Russian version of this video — only the narration language changes
